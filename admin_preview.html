Executive Summary

Five findings an administrator can act on, computed from the SAS-validated AAD2024-2904 universe (Department mode).

Most cross-department works per faculty member

Departments with 10+ roster faculty. Cross-department (“across”) distinct works divided by full faculty headcount — zero-collaboration faculty included in the denominator.

Fewest cross-department works per faculty member

Low is not bad by itself — some fields publish differently — but these are the places to ask why.
Methodology. Distinct-works counting throughout (a work counts once per department, however many people share it). “Cross-department” = the validated “across” relationship; “external” = collaborators at other institutions.

Department Scorecard

Raw counts hide size effects — a 133-person school will out-count a 20-person department at everything. This table normalizes per faculty member — using the full roster, including faculty with zero collaborations — so departments are comparable. Click a column header to sort.

Career-stage lens

Do early-career faculty collaborate across department lines, or is cross-department work concentrated among senior faculty?

Methodology. Faculty = full-roster headcount (2026-07-15 roster, 1,482 faculty incl. those with zero collaborations); % collaborating = share with at least one collaborative work. All per-faculty rates use the full-roster denominator. Multi-affiliated people count in each of their departments (validated person-centric convention), so faculty counts across rows sum to more than 1,482. *The Koch Institute is not a roster department; its Faculty cell shows active collaborators instead. Works are distinct works, not author-pair counts.

White Space — where collaboration should exist but doesn’t

Department pairs whose faculty work in overlapping disciplines but rarely (or never) publish together. High disciplinary similarity with low joint output is exactly where seed grants, joint appointments, and shared facilities tend to pay off fastest.

Methodology. Similarity = cosine similarity of the two departments’ faculty-discipline profiles (from each scholar’s discipline fields). Joint works = SAS-exact matrix cell for the pair. Score favors high similarity and low size-normalized joint output (normalized by full-roster faculty headcounts). Departments with fewer than 8 faculty excluded. MIT-WHOI pairs reflect the joint program’s structure — judge those with that context.

Retention Risk — who is hardest to replace?

For each department, the scholars whose departures would cost the most cross-department connectivity. Sole-coverage works counts the department’s cross-department works on which this person is the only member involved — if they leave, that bridge goes with them. A scholar with modest volume can still be a single point of failure.

Institution-wide: top sole-coverage bridge scholars

Methodology. Computed from the SAS-validated person work lists (person-centric convention). This complements the Network page’s Bridges view, which ranks by distinct-unit reach; sole-coverage measures irreplaceability instead of breadth. Neither is a performance measure — both identify structural dependence.
